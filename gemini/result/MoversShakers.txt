Today is Thursday, June fourth, two thousand twenty-six. Here is what the movers and shakers are noted as saying in the last twenty-four hours.

President Donald Trump has been making headlines regarding trade and foreign policy, recently threatening new tariffs of ten to twelve point five percent on sixty trading partners citing forced labor failures. The President also downplayed recent military strikes in Iran by stating that in that part of the world, a ceasefire is when you are shooting in a more moderate manner, all while signing new executive orders to vet powerful artificial intelligence models and reform the federal workforce. Working closely on these international fronts, Vice President J.D. Vance, acting as the lead in peace talks with Iran, stated that while a final deal is still to be determined, the current ceasefire is very much holding despite localized flare-ups. Vance also praised a new papal encyclical on artificial intelligence, agreeing that just war doctrines must be updated for the modern era. Meanwhile, Secretary of State Marco Rubio defended the administration's military actions during a House committee hearing, asserting that the recent operations degraded the Iranian defense industrial base, while also demanding significant reforms from European allies.

The administration's foreign policy has drawn sharp reactions across the media landscape. Tucker Carlson recently made a highly publicized break with the President over the Iran conflict, apologizing to his audience for previously supporting the administration and arguing that the current military campaign has abandoned America First principles, though he noted Vice President Vance is the only figure capable of carrying on the true legacy. Similarly, podcast host Joe Rogan used his platform to blast the war for depleting United States missile stockpiles, though he did express enthusiasm for a planned Ultimate Fighting Championship event on the White House lawn, calling it a perfectly on-brand spectacle for the President's upcoming eightieth birthday. Providing a satirical take, Jon Stewart mocked the President's late-night announcements of major combat operations from a Mar-a-Lago basement, comparing the atmosphere to the father of the bride settling up with the caterer, while using his podcast to warn about the long-term economic and energy security impacts of the conflict.

In Congress, Democratic leaders have been highly critical of the administration. House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries released a joint statement celebrating the passage of a War Powers Resolution meant to curb the President's military authority, condemning what he called a reckless and costly war of choice. Jeffries also took aim at the economy during a summit, calling it a disaster for everyday citizens dealing with rising costs, and strongly criticized the recent conduct of immigration enforcement as inhumane. Senate Democratic Leader Charles Schumer echoed these economic concerns, slamming the administration over recent inflation spikes of three point eight percent as a gut punch for American families, while also leading a legislative push to block a controversial one point eight billion dollar executive slush fund. Across the aisle, Republican Senator Josh Hawley focused on domestic relief, urging the President to approve a major federal disaster declaration for Missouri following devastating tornadoes, while pushing legislation to suspend the federal gas tax to provide immediate consumer relief.

On the progressive wing, Senator Bernie Sanders has been vocal about the rapid rise of technology, publishing arguments that artificial intelligence belongs to the people, not billionaires, and proposing public ownership of major tech firms to protect the working class. Sanders also introduced legislation to abolish super political action committees and eliminate student meal debt nationwide. Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez has similarly focused on corporate oversight, pressing environmental regulators on the impact of massive data center construction on local water supplies and launching a caucus dedicated to rooting out corporate influence. When recently asked about her own ambitions for a two thousand twenty-eight presidential run, she swerved direct confirmation, stating simply that her ambition is to change the country.

California Governor Gavin Newsom signed a law making it a felony to unlawfully seize election materials, a direct response to local ballot controversies, and issued executive orders to study job subsidies to mitigate tech sector layoffs caused by artificial intelligence. Michigan Governor Gretchen Whitmer generated significant buzz regarding the two thousand twenty-eight presidential race. After initially denying she would run, she walked back her comments hours later by saying never say never, though she stressed her current focus remains entirely on finishing her final term and balancing the state budget. Former Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg, widely seen as a frontrunner for the next Democratic primary, hinted at a future run by playfully asking allies to save him a seat for a campaign lunch, while actively campaigning for local candidates in conservative districts and calling for the government to deliver tangible results to regain public trust.

In Florida, Governor Ron DeSantis marked the start of the Atlantic hurricane season by launching a coalition to strengthen emergency management readiness. He also signed off on renaming a major airport after Donald Trump, calling it a great honor, while deflecting questions about a potential role in the federal cabinet by citing his unfinished business with property tax relief in Tallahassee. Fain has also been delivering speeches in Detroit slamming free trade and expressing solidarity with federal workers against administration policies.
