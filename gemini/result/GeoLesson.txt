Welcome to today's lesson. We begin our journey in the United States, specifically the rugged and mountainous state of Wyoming. Located in the Mountain West region, Wyoming is bordered by states like Montana to the north, Colorado to the south, and Idaho to the west. It is the tenth largest state by area, but the least populated, leaving vast stretches of untamed wilderness.

Wyoming is defined by the majestic Rocky Mountains, which cut right through the state. Here, you will find the awe-inspiring Grand Teton towering over the landscape, and the rugged Wind River Range. In the northwest corner lies Yellowstone National Park, sitting atop a volcanic hot spot that fuels its famous geysers and hot springs. Yellowstone Lake, located within the park, is the largest high-elevation lake in North America.

Several major rivers begin their journey in Wyoming's high peaks. The Snake River flows west towards the Pacific Ocean, while the Green River heads south to eventually join the Colorado River. The Yellowstone River flows north and east, carving beautiful canyons. 

This dramatic geography is central to Wyoming's identity and economy. The mountains and vast grassy plains support a strong ranching industry. Meanwhile, the incredible natural beauty of its peaks, valleys, and geothermal wonders drives a massive tourism industry, drawing millions of visitors eager to experience the wild heart of the American West.

For the second part of our lesson, we travel down to South America to explore the incredibly unique geography of Chile. If you look at a map, Chile appears as a long, narrow ribbon of land stretching along the western edge of the continent. It extends for over two thousand six hundred miles from north to south, but averages only about one hundred ten miles wide.

Chile is physically isolated from the rest of South America by two massive natural barriers. To the east, the towering Andes Mountains form the entire border with Argentina and Bolivia. To the west lies the vast, open expanse of the Pacific Ocean. 

Because of its extreme length, Chile experiences almost every climate imaginable. In the far north, you will find the Atacama Desert. It is considered the driest non-polar desert in the world, with some areas having no recorded rainfall in history. Moving south into the center of the country, the climate becomes mild and temperate. This fertile central valley, nestled between the coastal mountains and the high Andes, is where most of the population lives and where Chilean agriculture thrives. Finally, the deep south of Chile breaks apart into a rugged landscape of islands, deep ocean fjords, ancient glaciers, and cold alpine tundras.

Chile's geography deeply impacts its life and economy. The arid north holds some of the world's richest copper deposits, making mining vital. Meanwhile, the long Pacific coastline heavily influences its weather, provides rich fishing grounds, and supports essential global maritime trade.
