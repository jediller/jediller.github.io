Let us take a journey to the American West to explore the state of Colorado. Known as the Centennial State, Colorado is famous for its breathtaking elevation and diverse landscapes. It is a landlocked state bordered by Wyoming to the north, Nebraska and Kansas to the east, New Mexico and Oklahoma to the south, and Utah to the west. 

The most striking feature of Colorado's geography is the majestic Rocky Mountains, which run directly through the center of the state. This mountain range is home to Mount Elbert, the highest peak in the Rockies. Because of these mountains, Colorado serves as a crucial water tower for the western United States. Melting snowpack gives birth to several major rivers, including the mighty Colorado River, the Rio Grande, and the Arkansas River, which flow outward to supply water to millions of people across the country. 

But Colorado is not just mountains. The eastern third of the state is part of the High Plains, a flat and arid region that is deeply important for agriculture. Meanwhile, the western edge of the state features high desert landscapes, deep canyons, and plateaus. 

This varied geography is deeply tied to the state's economy and culture. The mountains draw millions of visitors for world-class skiing, hiking, and tourism, while the plains support farming and ranching. From high desert canyons to towering snowy peaks, Colorado is a beautiful showcase of nature's dramatic contrasts.

For our global geography lesson, we travel to Northern Europe to explore the beautiful country of Norway. Norway makes up the western and northern part of the Scandinavian Peninsula. It shares a long eastern border with Sweden, and touches Finland and Russia to the far northeast. However, its most defining geographical feature is its massive western coastline, which meets the North Sea, the Norwegian Sea, and the Barents Sea of the Arctic Ocean.

Norway is famous around the world for its fjords. These are deep, narrow inlets of the sea, with steep, towering cliffs on either side, carved by ancient ice age glaciers. The longest and deepest of these is the magnificent Sognefjord. Inland, the country is heavily mountainous, dominated by the rugged Scandinavian Mountains. Because of the steep terrain, large-scale farming is difficult, and much of the land is covered by bare rock, dense forests, and high plateaus.

Because of this challenging landscape, the geography of Norway has always pushed its people toward the ocean. The long coastline and deep-water ports have made Norway a historic leader in fishing, shipping, and maritime exploration. Today, the country's geography continues to be its greatest asset. The steep valleys and abundant rainfall provide perfect conditions for producing hydroelectric power, which supplies nearly all of the country's electricity. Norway's dramatic geography truly shapes every aspect of its modern life.
