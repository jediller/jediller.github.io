Today, we look at the beautiful state of Oregon, located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States. Oregon is bordered by the Pacific Ocean on its west, Washington to the north, Idaho to the east, and California and Nevada to the south. Its geography is incredibly diverse and dramatic. The mighty Columbia River forms much of its northern border, while the Snake River carves through deep canyons to define a large portion of its eastern boundary. Running north to south through the middle of the state is the spectacular Cascade Mountain Range. This rugged range is home to Mount Hood, Oregon's highest peak, as well as the breathtaking Crater Lake. Formed in the collapsed crater of an ancient volcano, Crater Lake is the deepest lake in the United States. 

The geography of Oregon plays a massive role in its climate and daily life. The Cascade Mountains create a powerful rain shadow effect. Because of this, the western side of the state, including the highly fertile Willamette Valley, receives plenty of rain, supporting dense green forests and rich agriculture. Meanwhile, the eastern side of the mountains transforms into a vast, dry high desert. The state's varied landscape supports major industries like timber, commercial fishing, farming, and outdoor tourism.

Next, we travel to South America to explore the striking geography of Chile. Chile is famously shaped like a long, narrow ribbon stretching along the western edge of the continent. It is boxed in by the towering Andes Mountains to the east, and the vast Pacific Ocean to the west. To the north lies Peru, and to the northeast is Bolivia, with Argentina running along its entire long eastern border. 

Chile's geography is defined by extreme and beautiful contrasts. In the far north, you will find the Atacama Desert, widely considered the driest non-polar desert in the entire world. The central region features a lush, fertile valley with a mild, Mediterranean climate. Moving into the deep south, the land shatters into a rugged, coastal landscape filled with alpine tundras, massive glaciers, dramatic fjords, and shimmering lakes.

This highly unique geography is incredibly important to the nation. The towering Andes Mountains act as a massive natural fortress, historically isolating Chile from the rest of the continent while providing immense mineral wealth, particularly copper, which drives much of the economy. Off the western coast, the cold Humboldt Current creates some of the richest commercial fishing grounds on the planet. Meanwhile, the protected, fertile central valley has turned Chile into an agricultural powerhouse, making it famous globally for its fruit and wine exports.
