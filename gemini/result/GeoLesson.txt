Today, we are exploring the geography of the great state of Colorado, located in the western region of the United States. Colorado is famous for its stunning and diverse landscape, heavily defined by the massive Rocky Mountains running directly through its center. Because of this, it boasts the highest average elevation of any state in the nation. Its highest peak is Mount Elbert, towering over fourteen thousand feet into the sky. 

However, Colorado is not entirely mountainous. The eastern third of the state is remarkably flat, covered by the High Plains, which are crucial for farming and agriculture. To the west, you will find the rugged canyons and desert lands of the Colorado Plateau. 

Colorado is incredibly important to the water supply of the United States. The Continental Divide runs along the crest of the Rockies. This means precipitation falling on the eastern side flows toward the Atlantic Ocean, while water on the western side flows toward the Pacific. Colorado is the birthplace of several major waterways, most notably the mighty Colorado River, which carves its way west to sustain much of the American Southwest. The state's dramatic and varied geography is also the lifeblood of its economy, drawing millions of visitors every year for world-class skiing, hiking, and outdoor adventures.

For our international lesson, we are traveling across the globe to the island nation of Japan, situated in East Asia. Japan is an expansive archipelago made up of nearly seven thousand islands, though the vast majority of its landmass comes from four main islands: Hokkaido in the north, Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu in the south. It is bordered by the Sea of Japan to the west and the vast Pacific Ocean to the east.

Japan's geography is famously rugged. Nearly three-quarters of the country is covered by densely forested mountains. The most iconic of these mountains is Mount Fuji, a beautifully symmetrical, dormant volcano and the highest point in Japan. 

This challenging geography has heavily shaped Japanese society. Because flat, livable land is so rare, the population is highly concentrated in coastal plains, creating some of the most densely populated urban areas on earth, such as Tokyo. Additionally, Japan is located squarely on the Pacific Ring of Fire. This geographic position makes the nation highly susceptible to earthquakes and volcanic activity, but it also blesses the country with thousands of natural, geothermal hot springs. Finally, being completely surrounded by oceans has historically isolated Japan from invasion, and has made the sea a primary source of both food and international trade for centuries.
