Today, we are looking at the geography of Colorado, a state located in the Mountain West region of the United States. Colorado is famous for its stunningly diverse landscape, which can be broadly divided into three main geographic areas.

Starting in the east, the state features the High Plains, a vast, flat expanse of prairie land that gradually rises in elevation as you move west. This agricultural heartland eventually gives way to the dramatic peaks of the Rocky Mountains, which cut directly through the center of the state. Colorado boasts more than fifty mountain peaks that exceed fourteen thousand feet in elevation, commonly referred to as "fourteeners." The highest of these is Mount Elbert. 

The western part of Colorado is characterized by the Colorado Plateau, a high desert landscape filled with deep canyons, mesas, and unique rock formations. Several major rivers originate in the snow-capped mountains of Colorado, most notably the Colorado River, the Arkansas River, and the Rio Grande. These waterways are incredibly important, not just for local agriculture, but for supplying water to the entire Southwestern United States. The state's geography, particularly the Rocky Mountains, is a massive driver for its economy, supporting a world-renowned outdoor recreation and ski industry.

Now, let's explore the geography of Norway, a beautiful Nordic country situated on the western half of the Scandinavian Peninsula in Northern Europe. Norway shares a long eastern border with Sweden, and touches Finland and Russia to the far northeast. 

Norway's geography is defined by its incredibly long and rugged coastline along the North Atlantic Ocean and the Barents Sea. This coastline is famous for its fjords, which are deep, narrow inlets of the sea, carved out by massive glaciers during the last ice age. The Sognefjord is the longest and deepest of these magnificent natural wonders.

Inland, Norway is highly mountainous. The Scandinavian Mountains run down the length of the country, creating a challenging terrain that has historically concentrated the population along the milder coastal areas. Because of these mountains, the country has an abundance of high plateaus and stunning waterfalls. It is also home to many large glaciers, including Jostedalsbreen, the largest glacier in continental Europe.

Norway's geography has deeply influenced its culture and economy. The extensive coastline and access to the sea made Norwegians expert seafarers and fishers throughout history. Today, the offshore geography of the North Sea is vital to the country's oil and natural gas industry, while its steep, mountainous terrain and abundant rainfall are perfectly suited for generating hydroelectric power, which supplies almost all of the nation's electricity.
