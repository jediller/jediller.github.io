Today, we are exploring the geography of Wyoming, a state located in the Mountain West sub-region of the western United States. Wyoming is a land of high elevation and dramatic contrasts. The western part of the state is dominated by the majestic Rocky Mountains, including the famous Teton Range and the Wind River Range. Here, you will find the awe-inspiring Yellowstone National Park, sitting atop a massive volcanic caldera that fuels its world-famous geysers and hot springs. 

The eastern part of Wyoming transitions into the High Plains, a vast expanse of shortgrass prairie that stretches toward the horizon. Running right through the state is the Continental Divide. Because of this, rivers in Wyoming flow in completely different directions. The Snake River winds its way toward the Pacific Ocean, while the Yellowstone and Green rivers eventually find their way to the Atlantic Ocean. 

Wyoming's rugged geography plays a huge role in its economy and lifestyle. The stunning mountains and national parks make tourism a vital industry, drawing millions of visitors eager to see the pristine wilderness. Meanwhile, the expansive eastern plains are essential for cattle ranching, a cornerstone of Wyoming's rich cowboy culture. Additionally, deep sedimentary basins scattered across the state hold vast reserves of coal, oil, and natural gas, making energy production a major economic driver.

For our international journey today, we travel to South America to explore the incredibly unique geography of Chile. Chile is famously known as the longest and narrowest country in the world. It stretches like a slender ribbon along the western edge of the continent. To its west crashes the vast Pacific Ocean, providing an incredibly long coastline. To its east, acting as a massive natural wall, are the towering Andes Mountains.

Because it extends so far from north to south, Chile contains a staggering variety of climates and landscapes. In the far north lies the Atacama Desert, widely considered one of the driest places on Earth. Moving south into the central region, the landscape shifts to a welcoming Mediterranean climate, characterized by fertile valleys that are world-renowned for agriculture and vineyards. Continuing further south, the geography breaks apart into a stunning, fragmented landscape of alpine tundras, deep fjords, ancient glaciers, and emerald lakes.

This extreme geography heavily influences life in Chile. The formidable Andes Mountains are packed with valuable minerals, making Chile the world's leading producer of copper. The long Pacific coastline sustains a massive fishing industry. Finally, because the country is naturally isolated by the desert to the north, the mountains to the east, and the ocean to the west and south, Chile enjoys a natural barrier against many pests and diseases, which greatly protects its thriving farming and wine industries.
