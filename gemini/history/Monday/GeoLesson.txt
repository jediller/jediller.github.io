Today, we will explore the geography of Wyoming, a state located in the western United States. Wyoming is a landlocked state where the majestic Rocky Mountains meet the sweeping High Plains. It is bordered by Montana, South Dakota, Nebraska, Colorado, Utah, and Idaho. The state is famous for its stunning mountain ranges, particularly the Tetons in the west and the Wind River Range. Wyoming is also home to Yellowstone, the world's first national park, which sits atop a volcanic hotspot, creating incredible geothermal features like the Old Faithful geyser. Important waterways include the Yellowstone River and the Green River, which carve through the rugged landscape. Because of its mountainous terrain and semi-arid climate, Wyoming is the least populous state in the nation. However, its unique geography is vital to its economy, driving a massive tourism industry, supporting extensive cattle ranching on the plains, and providing abundant natural resources like coal and natural gas.

Next, we journey to South Asia to explore the Kingdom of Bhutan. Bhutan is a small, landlocked country nestled high in the eastern Himalayas. It is sandwiched between two giant neighbors, bordered by China to the north and India to the south, east, and west. Bhutan's geography is defined by its extreme elevations. The towering Himalayas dominate the northern landscape, featuring dramatic peaks like Gangkhar Puensum, which is recognized as the highest unclimbed mountain in the world. Moving south, the terrain transitions into deep, lush valleys where the majority of the Bhutanese people live and farm. Powerful rivers, such as the Drangme Chhu, rush down from the glacial mountains through these valleys and eventually flow into India. In the far south, the land flattens out into sub-tropical plains. For centuries, Bhutan's steep and rugged geography acted as a natural fortress, isolating the country and allowing it to preserve its deeply rooted Buddhist culture. Today, the geography remains incredibly important, as those fast-flowing mountain rivers generate hydroelectric power, which is Bhutan's most valuable export.
