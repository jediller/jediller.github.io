Today we are looking at the state of Washington, located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States. It is bordered by the Pacific Ocean to the west, Oregon to the south, Idaho to the east, and the Canadian province of British Columbia to the north. 

Washington's geography is incredibly diverse, split down the middle by the towering Cascade Mountain Range. West of the Cascades, the climate is mild and wet, characterized by lush temperate rainforests, like the famous Hoh Rainforest, and the intricate, deep-water inlets of Puget Sound. This coastal geography is vital for the state's international trade and fishing industries. 

East of the Cascades, the landscape transforms dramatically into a drier, semi-arid region. This area features the fertile Columbia River Plateau. The mighty Columbia River, one of the state's most important waterways, winds through this region. It provides crucial hydroelectric power and irrigation that supports vast apple orchards and wheat fields. Major peaks in the state include Mount Rainier, a massive, active stratovolcano and the highest point in the state, which dominates the horizon near Seattle. The diverse geography of Washington not only shapes its local climates but also drives its economy, supporting agriculture, maritime trade, and a massive outdoor recreation industry.

For our international destination, we are exploring New Zealand, an island country located in the southwestern Pacific Ocean. It consists mainly of two large landmasses: the North Island and the South Island, which are separated by the narrow Cook Strait. The country is famous for its dramatic and highly varied topography, shaped over millions of years by tectonic uplift and volcanic activity.

The South Island is dominated by the Southern Alps, a spectacular mountain range running along its spine, featuring the country's highest peak, Aoraki Mount Cook. This southern region is famous for deep, breathtaking fjords, such as Milford Sound, which were carved by ancient glaciers. It also features large alpine lakes like Lake Wakatipu. 

The North Island is generally less mountainous but is home to a highly active volcanic zone. Here, you will find Lake Taupo, the country's largest lake, which actually sits inside a massive volcanic caldera. The North Island is also home to the Waikato River, New Zealand's longest river. The nation's isolated island geography has allowed a completely unique ecosystem to evolve, with many native bird and plant species found nowhere else on Earth. Its beautifully varied landscapes, ranging from rolling green pastures to rugged mountain peaks, are central to the nation's cultural identity and support a world-renowned eco-tourism industry.
