Today, we explore the geography of Oregon, located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States. Oregon is bordered by the Pacific Ocean to the west, Washington to the north, Idaho to the east, and California and Nevada to the south. Its landscape is incredibly diverse. Starting from the west, you will find a rugged coastline that gives way to the dense forests of the Coast Range mountains. Just inland lies the fertile Willamette Valley, carved by the Willamette River, where most of the state's population lives. Moving further east, the terrain rises dramatically into the Cascade Mountain Range. This range is famous for its towering volcanic peaks, including Mount Hood, and the stunning Crater Lake, which is the deepest lake in the United States. Beyond the Cascades, the eastern half of Oregon shifts into a vast, dry, high desert region. Major waterways heavily influence the state. The massive Columbia River forms much of its northern border, carving out the spectacular Columbia River Gorge, while the Snake River defines its eastern edge, flowing through the deep Hells Canyon. Oregon's geography is deeply tied to its economy. Heavy rainfall in the west supports a massive timber industry, the rich valleys provide excellent agricultural land, and the mighty rivers generate essential hydroelectric power for the entire region.

Moving on to our international destination, we travel to South America to explore the incredible geography of Chile. Chile is famously known as the longest and narrowest country in the world. It stretches like a long ribbon down the western edge of the continent, locked between the towering Andes Mountains to the east and the vast Pacific Ocean to the west. This extreme shape gives Chile a massive variety of climates and landscapes. In the far north lies the Atacama Desert, known as the driest non-polar desert on Earth. Some weather stations there have never recorded a single drop of rain. Moving south, you reach the fertile Central Valley. This area enjoys a mild Mediterranean climate, making it the agricultural heartland of the country and home to the capital city, Santiago. Continuing into the southern region, the landscape breaks apart into a rugged, beautiful expanse of alpine tundras, deep fjords, glaciers, and coastal islands, eventually ending at Cape Horn. Chile's geography plays a major role in its survival and prosperity. The mighty Andes Mountains are packed with mineral wealth, making Chile one of the world's top producers of copper. Meanwhile, the cold Humboldt Current in the Pacific Ocean brings nutrient-rich waters to the coast, supporting a massive fishing industry. Finally, the natural boundaries of the desert, mountains, and ocean have protected the country from many agricultural pests, allowing its farming and famous wine industries to thrive.
