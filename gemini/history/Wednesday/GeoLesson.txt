Today we are looking at the midwestern United States, specifically the state of Michigan. Michigan has a completely unique geography because it is split into two distinct land masses known as the Upper Peninsula and the Lower Peninsula, which are separated by the Straits of Mackinac. 

Michigan is defined by water. In fact, it is the only state to border four of the five Great Lakes: Lake Superior, Lake Michigan, Lake Huron, and Lake Erie. Because of this, it has the longest freshwater coastline of any political subdivision in the world. Wherever you stand in the state, you are never more than six miles from a natural water source, and never more than eighty-five miles from a Great Lake. 

The Lower Peninsula is shaped like a mitten and is generally flat with rolling hills, particularly in the northern half. The Upper Peninsula is more rugged, heavily forested, and contains the Porcupine Mountains in the west. 

This unique, water-rich geography has historically made Michigan a massive hub for trade, shipping, and industry. The easy transport of raw materials like iron ore from the Upper Peninsula down through the Great Lakes was crucial to the growth of the automotive industry in cities like Detroit. Today, its dense forests and thousands of inland lakes make it a major destination for outdoor recreation and tourism.

Moving to the global stage, let us explore the geography of Chile, located on the western edge of South America. Chile is famous for its extreme shape. It is a long, incredibly narrow ribbon of land that stretches over two thousand six hundred miles from north to south, but averages only one hundred ten miles from east to west.

This unique shape is bounded by two massive natural barriers: the towering Andes Mountains along its entire eastern border with Argentina, and the vast Pacific Ocean to its west. 

Because it stretches so far from north to south, Chile contains a staggering variety of landscapes. In the far north lies the Atacama Desert, considered the driest non-polar desert in the world. Moving south, the terrain transitions into a fertile central valley, which enjoys a Mediterranean climate perfect for agriculture. Further south, the landscape splinters into the rugged, icy wilderness of Patagonia, featuring deep fjords, ancient glaciers, and dramatic mountain peaks.

Chile's geography is highly important to its economy and way of life. The arid north contains massive, easily accessible deposits of copper and lithium, making mining a critical industry. Meanwhile, the central valleys provide rich agricultural output like fruit and wine, and the incredibly long coastline supports a robust and vital fishing industry.
