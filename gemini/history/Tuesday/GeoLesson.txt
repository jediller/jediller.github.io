Today's featured United States geography lesson takes us to the Pacific Northwest, exploring the wonderfully diverse state of Oregon. Oregon is located on the western edge of the country, bordered by the vast Pacific Ocean to the west, Washington to the north, Idaho to the east, and California and Nevada to the south. 

The state's geography is dramatically divided by the towering Cascade Mountain Range, which runs north to south. This mountain chain features breathtaking dormant volcanoes, most notably Mount Hood, and creates a massive weather barrier known as a rain shadow. Because of this, the western third of the state is heavily forested, green, and receives abundant rainfall, while the eastern two-thirds is dominated by the dry, arid landscapes of the High Desert. 

Nestled between the coastal mountains and the Cascades is the Willamette Valley. This incredibly fertile lowland region is the agricultural heart of the state and holds the vast majority of Oregon's population. To the north, the mighty Columbia River carves a deep, scenic gorge and forms much of the border with Washington state. This river is a vital geographic and economic lifeline, providing immense hydroelectric power and serving as a major shipping channel. Oregon is also famous for Crater Lake. Located in the south, it is the deepest lake in the United States, filled with pure blue water inside the sunken crater of an ancient volcano. 

Now, let us travel across the globe to the vibrant nation of Morocco, located in the far northwestern corner of Africa. Morocco boasts a highly strategic geographic position, featuring coastlines on the Atlantic Ocean to the west and the Mediterranean Sea to the north. These two massive bodies of water connect at the Strait of Gibraltar, a narrow but globally important waterway that separates Morocco from Spain and the rest of the European continent.

Moving inland from the fertile coastal plains along the Atlantic, where the majority of the nation's food is grown, the landscape dramatically rises into the Atlas Mountains. This massive, rugged mountain system dominates the center of the country and is divided into the High Atlas, the Middle Atlas, and the Anti-Atlas ranges. These mountains act as a towering natural wall, catching moisture from the ocean winds and feeding important agricultural waterways, such as the Sebou River. 

Beyond the soaring peaks to the south and east lies the vast, golden expanse of the Sahara Desert. Historically, this immense, harsh desert isolated Morocco from the rest of the African continent, which naturally steered the country's historic trade and cultural ties northward toward the Mediterranean and Europe. Today, Morocco's spectacular geography of sunny coastlines, snow-capped mountains, and deep desert dunes plays a critical role in its booming tourism industry, while its coastal plains sustain its people.
