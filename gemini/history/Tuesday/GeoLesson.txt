Today we are looking at the geography of the US state of Idaho. Located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States, Idaho is a landlocked state known for its rugged and mountainous landscapes. It shares a short border with Canada to the north and is surrounded by six other US states, including Washington and Oregon to the west, and Montana and Wyoming to the east. A major feature of Idaho is the spectacular Rocky Mountains, which cover much of the state and provide vast forests and deep river canyons. The most famous of these canyons is Hells Canyon, carved by the Snake River. Hells Canyon is actually deeper than the Grand Canyon. 

The Snake River is the most important river in Idaho. It curves across the southern part of the state through a large, wide valley known as the Snake River Plain. This plain is incredibly important for the state's economy. The soil here, enriched by ancient volcanic ash, combined with irrigation water from the Snake River, creates the perfect conditions for growing crops, most notably Idaho's famous potatoes. In the northern part of the state, you will find beautiful, deep lakes left behind by ancient glaciers, surrounded by thick evergreen forests. From towering snowy peaks to deep river gorges and fertile plains, Idaho's geography shapes both its incredible natural beauty and its strong agricultural industry.

For our world geography lesson, we travel to the country of Morocco. Morocco is located in the northwest corner of Africa. It has a very special location because it has coastlines on both the Atlantic Ocean to the west and the Mediterranean Sea to the north. Just across the narrow Strait of Gibraltar to the north is the country of Spain in Europe. 

Morocco's geography is defined by three main features: its long coastlines, its towering mountains, and the vast desert. Running right through the middle of the country are the Atlas Mountains. This massive mountain range separates the mild, wet coastal areas from the harsh, dry climate of the Sahara Desert in the south. The highest peak is Mount Toubkal, which is the tallest mountain in North Africa and is often covered in snow. To the south and east of the mountains lies the famous Sahara Desert, a massive landscape of rolling sand dunes and rocky plateaus. 

Several rivers flow from the Atlas Mountains toward the ocean, providing crucial water for drinking and farming in the valleys and coastal plains. Morocco's geography is highly important to its way of life. The coastlines support fishing and international trade, the mountains provide water and minerals, and the distinct separation created by the Atlas Mountains gives Morocco a unique mix of mild Mediterranean and extreme desert environments.
