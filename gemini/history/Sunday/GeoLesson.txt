Today we explore Pennsylvania, located in the Northeastern and Mid-Atlantic regions of the United States. Pennsylvania is uniquely positioned geographically. While it is completely landlocked from the open ocean, it is bordered by the Delaware River to the east, providing crucial access to the Atlantic. To the northwest, a small panhandle gives the state a shoreline on Lake Erie, connecting it to the Great Lakes network. 

The state is dominated by the Appalachian Mountains, which run diagonally through its center, creating a stunning landscape of rolling ridges and deep valleys. Major waterways define its regions. The Susquehanna River flows through the heart of the state down into the Chesapeake Bay, while the mighty Ohio River is born in the city of Pittsburgh, right where the Allegheny and Monongahela rivers meet. 

Pennsylvania's geography has been vital to its history and economy. The rugged Appalachian region is incredibly rich in coal and natural gas, which famously fueled the American industrial revolution and its massive steel industry. Meanwhile, the fertile, gently rolling plains in the southeastern part of the state provide excellent agricultural land that continues to support a robust farming economy today.

For our international destination today, we travel to Namibia, a country situated on the southwestern coast of Africa. Namibia boasts some of the most striking and extreme geography on the planet. Its entire western edge is bordered by the South Atlantic Ocean, featuring the infamous Skeleton Coast. This coastline gets its name from the harsh ocean currents and thick fogs that have caused countless shipwrecks over the centuries. 

Right along this coast lies the Namib Desert, one of the oldest and driest deserts in the world. It is famous for its massive, towering red sand dunes that stretch endlessly and sometimes plunge directly into the ocean waves. Moving inland from the coast, the terrain rises sharply into a high central plateau. This central highland is where you will find the capital city of Windhoek and the rugged Naukluft Mountains. Further east, the landscape transitions into the Kalahari Desert, a vast, sandy savanna shared with neighboring Botswana. Namibia's major rivers, like the Orange River in the deep south and the Kunene River in the far north, form natural borders with its neighbors. 

This stark, arid geography deeply impacts life in Namibia. While the lack of steady rainfall makes large-scale agriculture very difficult, the dramatic landscapes support a booming eco-tourism industry and incredible wildlife conservation areas. Furthermore, the ancient desert geology is packed with valuable natural resources, especially diamonds, which are a cornerstone of the national economy.
