Today we are looking at the geography of the US state of Oregon. Located in the Pacific Northwest region of the Western United States, Oregon boasts a highly diverse landscape. To its west, the state is bordered by the vast Pacific Ocean, featuring a rugged and beautiful coastline. Moving inland, you encounter the Coast Range mountains, heavily forested and rich in timber, which historically fueled the state's economy. Further east lies the fertile Willamette Valley, carved by the Willamette River. This valley is the agricultural heart of Oregon and home to most of its population. 

Dominating the center of the state is the majestic Cascade Range, a string of volcanic peaks including Mount Hood, the state's highest point, and the ancient caldera holding the stunningly deep blue Crater Lake. The northern border of Oregon is largely defined by the powerful Columbia River, a major waterway crucial for transportation, salmon fishing, and hydroelectric power. The eastern portion of the state shifts dramatically into the high desert of the Great Basin, characterized by arid lands, distinct rock formations, and wide-open spaces. This diverse geography supports a wide range of industries, from logging and farming to outdoor recreation and tourism.

Next, we turn our attention to the global stage and explore the geography of Morocco. Situated in the northwestern corner of Africa, Morocco occupies a highly strategic location. It is bordered by the Atlantic Ocean to its west and the Mediterranean Sea to its north, separated from Europe by the narrow Strait of Gibraltar. This extensive coastline provides critical access for trade and a thriving fishing industry. 

Inland, Morocco is defined by its dramatic mountain ranges. The rugged Rif Mountains run parallel to the Mediterranean coast, while the magnificent Atlas Mountains cut diagonally across the country. The High Atlas range features Mount Toubkal, the highest peak in North Africa. These mountains are vital, capturing rainfall and winter snow that feed the country's major rivers, like the Sebou, and provide water for the fertile coastal plains where much of Morocco's agriculture takes place. Beyond the Atlas Mountains to the south and east, the landscape transitions rapidly into the harsh, arid expanse of the Sahara Desert. The Draa River, Morocco's longest, flows from the High Atlas down toward the desert sands. Morocco's varied geography, from sweeping coastlines and snow-capped peaks to endless desert dunes, creates a breathtaking environment and fundamentally shapes its modern economy.
