Today we are looking at the great state of Washington, located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States. Washington's geography is defined by incredible contrasts, largely thanks to the Cascade Mountain Range that splits the state right down the middle. 

If you stand on the western side of the Cascades, you will find lush, green environments. This area borders the Pacific Ocean and includes the beautiful Puget Sound, a complex system of interconnected coastal waterways. It is also home to the rugged Olympic Mountains and one of the only temperate rainforests in the country. 

However, if you cross to the eastern side of the Cascades, the landscape completely changes. The high mountains block the rain coming from the ocean, creating a dry, arid climate that is heavily reliant on irrigation. This eastern agricultural hub is nourished by the mighty Columbia River, the largest river in the Pacific Northwest, which winds its way down to the Pacific Ocean. 

Looming over the state is Mount Rainier, a massive, glacier-capped active volcano that stands as the highest peak in the Cascade Range. Washington's geography is not just beautiful; it is the economic engine of the state, powering a massive agricultural and farming industry in the dry east, and supporting major shipping, fishing, and technology ports in the wetter west.

For our international journey, we are exploring the geography of Chile, a fascinating country located along the western edge of South America. Chile has one of the most unusual and extreme shapes of any nation on Earth. It is a long, narrow ribbon stretching over two thousand six hundred miles from north to south, but it averages only about one hundred ten miles wide from east to west.

This unique shape is defined by two massive geographical barriers. To the east, the towering, snow-capped peaks of the Andes Mountains form a strict natural border with Argentina. To the west, the country drops off into the vast, deep expanse of the Pacific Ocean. 

Because it spans such a long distance from north to south, Chile contains an amazing variety of climates. In the far north, you will find the Atacama Desert, widely considered the driest non-polar desert in the entire world. Moving south into the center of the country, the climate becomes Mediterranean, featuring rich valleys perfect for growing grapes and other crops. Continuing further south, the land shatters into deep ocean fjords, towering glaciers, and the spectacular alpine lakes of the Patagonia region. 

Chile's dramatic geography heavily influences its economy and way of life. The Andes Mountains are packed with valuable minerals, making Chile a global leader in copper mining, while the cold Pacific ocean currents provide a rich environment for one of the world's most productive fishing industries.
