Today we are looking at the geography of the US state of Wyoming. Located in the Mountain West subregion of the Western United States, Wyoming is defined by its dramatic and rugged terrain. It is the tenth largest state by area, but the least populated, largely due to its vast mountainous landscapes and high elevation plains. The state is dominated by various ranges of the Rocky Mountains, including the majestic Grand Tetons in the west and the Wind River Range. 

Wyoming is famous for Yellowstone National Park, home to supervolcanoes, geysers like Old Faithful, and the beautiful Yellowstone Lake. Major rivers include the Snake River, the Green River, and the Yellowstone River, which carve deep valleys and provide essential water sources. This rugged geography is incredibly important to Wyoming's economy and identity. The mountainous and volcanic regions drive a massive tourism industry, while the sprawling, high-altitude plains are perfect for cattle ranching. Beneath the surface, the state's geology holds vast reserves of coal, natural gas, and oil, making mining and energy extraction a cornerstone of local life.

Next, we travel to the continent of Africa to explore the geography of Namibia. Located on the southwestern coast of Africa, Namibia is bordered by the Atlantic Ocean to the west. It is a land of extreme contrasts and striking desert landscapes. The country gets its name from the Namib Desert, one of the oldest and driest deserts in the world, which stretches along the entire Atlantic coastline. This coastal desert features massive, towering red sand dunes that meet the crashing waves of the ocean.

Further inland, the landscape rises to a central plateau before sloping down into the Kalahari Desert in the east. In the south, the Fish River Canyon cuts deeply through the earth, creating one of the largest canyons in the world, second only to the Grand Canyon. The Orange River forms the southern border with South Africa, providing a rare continuous source of water in an otherwise arid region. Namibia's harsh, dry geography means it is one of the most sparsely populated countries on Earth. However, the geology is extremely rich. The coastal sands and inland rocks are packed with valuable minerals, especially diamonds and uranium, which are vital to the national economy. Additionally, the unique, untouched desert ecosystems draw wildlife enthusiasts and photographers from all over the globe.
