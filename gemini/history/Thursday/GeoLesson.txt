Today, we are looking at the geography of Idaho. Located in the Pacific Northwest region of the United States, Idaho is a landlocked state famous for its rugged, mountainous landscapes. It shares a short, northern border with the Canadian province of British Columbia. The dominant geographical features are the heavily forested ranges of the Rocky Mountains, which cover most of the state from the central region up into the northern panhandle. The jagged Bitterroot Range creates a massive, natural border with Montana to the east. 

In the southern part of the state, the geography changes dramatically. Here, you will find the Snake River Plain, a wide, flat, and crescent-shaped valley. The mighty Snake River carves its way through this plain, plunging over the spectacular Shoshone Falls, which are actually taller than Niagara Falls. This river is the absolute lifeblood of Idaho. Its waters provide essential irrigation for the rich, volcanic soil of the plain, making it possible to grow the state's most famous agricultural export, the potato. Idaho also boasts stunning lakes in its northern panhandle, including the deep, glacier-carved Lake Pend Oreille. 

For our international destination today, we are traveling to South America to explore the geography of Chile. Chile is incredibly unique in its shape. It is a long, narrow ribbon of land stretching over two thousand six hundred miles along the western edge of the continent, yet it averages only about one hundred ten miles in width. This creates a dramatic geographic sandwich. To the east, forming the border with Argentina and Bolivia, are the towering, snow-capped peaks of the Andes Mountains. To the west, the entire length of the country is bounded by the vast Pacific Ocean. 

Because it spans so many lines of latitude, Chile experiences almost every major climate found on Earth. In the far north lies the Atacama Desert, famous for being the driest non-polar desert in the world. Moving south, the landscape transitions into a fertile central valley with a mild, Mediterranean climate. This region is where most of the population lives, and where Chile's famous agricultural and wine industries thrive. Finally, the deep south shatters into a cold, rugged landscape of deep ocean fjords, ancient glaciers, and alpine islands. Chile's dramatic geography is vital to its survival. The Andes are incredibly rich in copper, driving the national economy, while the long coastline supports a massive fishing industry. Together, the mountains and the ocean act as natural barriers that have deeply shaped the nation's history and culture.
