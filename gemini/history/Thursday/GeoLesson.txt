Today we are looking at the great state of New Mexico, located in the southwestern region of the United States. New Mexico is often called the Land of Enchantment, and its geography certainly lives up to the name. It shares borders with Colorado to the north, Oklahoma and Texas to the east, Texas and the country of Mexico to the south, and Arizona to the west.

The landscape is incredibly diverse. In the north, you will find the southern tip of the Rocky Mountains, featuring the Sangre de Cristo range. Here, the state's highest point, Wheeler Peak, rises over thirteen thousand feet into the sky. As you move south and west, the terrain shifts into high deserts, expansive mesas, and the remarkable White Sands National Park, famous for its dazzling, white gypsum sand dunes. 

Water is a precious resource in this arid environment. The mighty Rio Grande flows straight down the middle of the state from north to south, acting as a vital lifeline for agriculture and cities like Albuquerque. To the east, the Pecos River carves its way through the plains.

New Mexico's geography has deeply shaped its history and economy. The fertile valleys along the rivers support farming, while the mountainous and desert regions are rich in minerals, oil, and natural gas. The beautiful and varied terrain also makes it an amazing destination for nature lovers.

Now, let us travel across the globe to the island nation of Madagascar. Located in the Indian Ocean, just across the Mozambique Channel from the southeastern coast of Africa, Madagascar is the fourth largest island in the entire world. 

Because it has been isolated from the African mainland for millions of years, Madagascar's geography has fostered an incredibly unique environment. A steep ridge of mountains runs down the center of the island from north to south, known as the central highlands. This elevated region enjoys a cooler climate and is heavily terraced for growing rice, which is the staple food of the country.

To the east of these mountains, the land drops sharply down to a narrow coastal strip. This eastern region receives heavy rainfall from ocean winds, creating lush, dense tropical rainforests. Moving to the western side of the mountains, the landscape is much drier, featuring rolling plains and unique forests. Further south, the environment turns into a harsh, spiny desert.

Madagascar is completely surrounded by the warm waters of the Indian Ocean. The island's major rivers, such as the Betsiboka, flow westward from the highlands toward the sea, often carrying bright red soil that makes the water look like flowing rust. This incredible geographical variety is absolutely vital to the country, as it supports agriculture and is home to amazing wildlife, like lemurs, that are found nowhere else on earth.
