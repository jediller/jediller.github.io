Today we journey to the Pacific Northwest to explore the dramatic geography of Washington State. Washington is famously divided in half by the towering Cascade Mountain Range. To the west of the Cascades, the land meets the Pacific Ocean and the deep saltwater inlets of Puget Sound. This western side is lush and green, home to the Olympic Peninsula and the incredibly wet Hoh Rainforest. 

But if you cross the snow-capped peaks of the Cascades, which include the massive, glacier-covered volcano Mount Rainier, the landscape changes completely. The eastern half of Washington is actually a dry, arid basin. This stark difference is caused by a rain shadow effect, where the tall mountains block the wet storms rolling in from the ocean. 

The mighty Columbia River snakes through this eastern desert, carving deep gorges and eventually forming much of the state's southern border with Oregon. This river is the absolute lifeblood of the region. It provides vital irrigation that turns the dry eastern plains into some of the most productive agricultural land in the world, making the state famous for its apples and wheat. The river's powerful flow is also harnessed by giant hydroelectric dams, providing immense power to the region. From lush coastlines to rugged peaks and sun-drenched plains, Washington's striking geography dictates its climate, economy, and way of life.

Now we travel to the western edge of South America to look at the beautifully extreme geography of Chile. Chile is famous for its highly unusual shape. It is a long, ribbon-like country, stretching over two thousand six hundred miles from north to south, but averaging only about one hundred ten miles in width. This narrow strip of land is tightly squeezed between the towering Andes Mountains to the east and the vast Pacific Ocean to the west. 

Because it is so unbelievably long from top to bottom, Chile experiences almost every climate imaginable. In the far north lies the Atacama Desert, widely considered the driest non-polar desert in the entire world. Moving down the coast, the center of the country features a comfortable, mild climate, filled with fertile, green valleys that make Chile a global powerhouse in agriculture and wine-making. Continuing further south, the land fractures into a wild, rugged region of deep coastal fjords, ancient blue glaciers, and freezing alpine tundras. 

Chile's dramatic geography acts like a natural fortress. The massive, impassable Andes Mountains and the wide Pacific Ocean have historically isolated the country. This isolation has protected its agriculture from foreign pests and created truly unique environments. Rich in copper in the northern deserts and rich in crops in the central valleys, Chile is a breathtaking land of sheer geographical contrasts.
