Today, we begin our geography journey in the Midwestern United States with the state of Kansas. Situated right in the heart of the country, Kansas is landlocked, sharing its borders with Nebraska, Missouri, Oklahoma, and Colorado. While many people picture Kansas as perfectly flat, its geography is actually a gentle, rolling slope that drops in elevation from the High Plains in the west down to the river valleys in the east. 

One of the state's most unique geographic features is the Flint Hills in eastern Kansas. Because the rocky, flint-filled soil here was too difficult for early settlers to plow, this area was never heavily farmed. As a result, it preserves one of the last remaining large tracts of unplowed tallgrass prairie in North America. Water also plays a vital role in the state's landscape. The northeastern border is carved out by the winding Missouri River, while the Kansas and Arkansas Rivers flow steadily across the central and southern plains. Although Kansas has almost no natural lakes, it has constructed numerous large reservoirs, such as Milford Lake, to help manage water and prevent flooding. This rolling, open geography is deeply tied to the state's identity and economy, providing the perfect environment for massive agricultural production, especially vast, golden fields of wheat and sprawling cattle ranches.

Next, we travel south to the western coast of South America to explore the fascinating geography of Peru. Peru is a country of extreme physical contrasts, bordered by the vast Pacific Ocean to the west. The nation is generally divided into three very distinct geographic zones. 

First is the coastal region, a narrow and highly arid desert plain running alongside the ocean. Despite receiving almost no rainfall, this region is home to the capital city of Lima and sustains agriculture using water from dozens of short rivers flowing down from the mountains. Just inland, the terrain rises dramatically into the mighty Andes Mountain range. The Andes dominate the center of the country and feature breathtaking, snow-capped peaks, including Mount Huascaran, Peru's highest mountain. Tucked high in these southern mountains sits the massive Lake Titicaca, which Peru shares with Bolivia. It is famous globally for being the highest navigable lake in the world. 

Finally, dropping down the eastern slopes of the Andes, you enter the lush, dense Amazon Rainforest. This vibrant jungle region makes up more than half of the country's land area. In fact, the mighty Amazon River itself originates high in the Peruvian Andes before winding its way east into the dense canopy. Peru's incredibly varied geography creates highly diverse climates and ecosystems, shaping everything from its rich mineral mining industries in the highlands to its ancient historical sites, like Machu Picchu, which were built directly into the steep mountain ridges.
